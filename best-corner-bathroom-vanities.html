Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Measure the corner before anything else. Note the wall length on both sides, the door swing, and how far drawers need to pull out. A 36-inch vanity that fits the wall can still block a door.
  • Floating or freestanding. Wall-mounted (floating) vanities make a small room feel bigger but must be screwed into studs; freestanding cabinets are easier to install and hold more weight.
  • Material affects lifespan in a wet room. All seven cabinets here use MDF or engineered wood, which holds up in a bathroom as long as you keep the finish sealed and wipe up standing water.
  • Check where your plumbing sits. You often have to notch the drawers around the drain; door-front cabinets give you more room to work if your supply lines sit high or off-center.
  • Price is not the whole cost. Most of these ship ready to assemble, so budget an hour or two of your time, and factor in a faucet if one is not included.

In most bathrooms the corner is the most wasted square footage. The sink lands on the long wall, a trash can and a cobweb fill the corner, and the room feels tighter than it should. Size a vanity to fit that corner and you flip it: the plumbing moves out of your walking path and the floor opens up.

We spent time comparing seven compact and floating vanities that suit corner and small-bathroom layouts, ranging from a $199 30-inch cabinet to a $789 48-inch floating unit. We looked at footprint, storage, build quality, and how forgiving each one is to install in a real, plumbed-in bathroom rather than a showroom.

The one we would buy for most people is the DELUXE LIVING 36 Inch vanity. It hits the sweet spot between storage and footprint, and the cabinet feels sturdier than its $349.99 price suggests. If your corner cannot take 36 inches, the 30-inch LIKIMIO is the runner-up; if you want the open, floating look, the eclife 36-inch and the splurge-worthy Ambrovina 48-inch are both worth a look.

How We Picked

We started by limiting the field to vanities that make sense in a corner or small bathroom: cabinets 48 inches and under, with a footprint that will not dominate a tight room. From there we prioritized real storage (drawers and cabinets you can reach around the plumbing), a finish that holds up to humidity, and a cabinet that ships with a usable top and basin rather than leaving you to source one. We set a hard ceiling on flimsy particleboard builds and skipped anything with a pattern of owner complaints about warping, sagging shelves, or hardware that strips on the first install.

How We Tested

We combined a hands-on look at comparable cabinets with a close read of the specs and verified owner reviews. For each vanity we checked the stated dimensions against the storage layout to see how much usable space survives once you account for the drain and supply lines, weighed the wall-mounting requirements against typical bathroom framing, and tracked how owners described assembly, finish durability, and water resistance after several months of use. The ranking comes down to how each cabinet performs in the kind of small, fully plumbed bathroom these vanities end up in.

The Competition

We considered several other small-format vanities that did not make the final list. True triangular corner units, the kind that bolt diagonally across the corner itself, look space-efficient on paper, but most we reviewed had tiny, odd-shaped basins and almost no usable storage, which defeats the purpose if you keep anything at all in your bathroom.

We also passed on the cheapest particleboard cabinets under $150. They undercut even our Vabches pick on price, but owner reviews kept flagging swelling around the sink cutout and hardware that stripped on the first assembly, the failure mode you do not want in a humid room.

Several oversized 60-inch and double-sink vanities were strong cabinets in their own right, yet they do not belong in a corner or small bathroom, so they fell outside the scope of this guide. If you have the wall space for one, our other roundups cover those sizes in detail.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are corner bathroom vanities a good idea for small bathrooms?

Yes. A corner or compact vanity tucks the sink into space that would otherwise go unused, which frees up the center of the room and makes a small bathroom feel larger. Measure the corner before you buy, including how far the door and any drawers swing open.

What size vanity fits a small or corner bathroom?

A 24- to 36-inch vanity fits most small and corner bathrooms. The picks in this guide run from 30 to 48 inches; a 30-inch model like the LIKIMIO or Vabches suits the tightest spaces, while a 36-inch model like our DELUXE LIVING pick works when you have a little more wall to spare.

Are floating vanities good for small bathrooms?

Floating (wall-mounted) vanities make a small bathroom feel more open because you can see the floor underneath, which tricks the eye into reading more space. The trade-off is that they must be anchored into studs or solid blocking and will not hold as much weight as a freestanding cabinet.
