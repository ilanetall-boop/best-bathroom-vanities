Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Measure the wall, the door swing, and the toilet clearance before you shop. Width gets all the attention, but the standard 21-inch depth is what usually trips people up in a tight room.
  • Find your plumbing rough-in first. Where the water lines and drain come out of the wall or floor decides whether a vanity drops in easily or turns into a plumbing job.
  • Check whether the countertop, sink, and faucet are included. Some vanities are complete units; others are cabinet-only, and the difference can be a few hundred dollars.
  • Freestanding is the easy install; floating (wall-mounted) looks lighter and cleans up easier but needs studs or solid blocking to hold the weight.
  • RTA (ready-to-assemble) vanities cost less and ship in flat boxes, but you build them. Pre-assembled costs more and arrives ready to set in place.

A bathroom vanity is one of those purchases that looks simple until you are standing in the room with a tape measure and a dozen browser tabs open. It is the largest single thing most people put in a bathroom, it has to play nicely with your plumbing, and getting the width or depth wrong by even an inch or two can mean a return, a restocking fee, and another week of living with a hole where your sink should be.

The good news is that the decision comes down to a short list of factors, and once you know what they are, the choice gets a lot less intimidating. The short version: measure your space first, locate your plumbing, decide whether you need one sink or two, and only then start falling in love with finishes. Style matters, but it is the easiest part to get right and the cheapest to change later.

What You Need to Know

Every vanity listing describes itself with the same handful of numbers, so it helps to know what they mean. Width is the dimension everyone leads with, and it ranges from about 18 inches for a powder-room unit to 72 inches for a generous double. Depth is the measurement from the wall to the front edge, and it is almost always around 21 inches; the slim exceptions, around 15 to 18 inches, exist specifically for narrow rooms. Height runs 32 to 36 inches finished, with 36 inches (often called "comfort height") now the more common standard because it saves your back.

The next thing to pin down is what actually comes in the box. A vanity can mean the cabinet alone, or a complete set with the countertop, an integrated or drop-in sink, and the holes pre-drilled for a faucet. Faucet hole spacing matters here: single-hole, 4-inch centerset, and 8-inch widespread are not interchangeable, so the top has to match the faucet you want.

Finally, look at how the cabinet is built. Drawers versus doors changes how usable the storage is, especially around the plumbing trap, which eats into the space behind a center door. Solid wood and plywood boxes hold up to bathroom humidity far better than particleboard or MDF, and a quality finish is what keeps moisture from swelling the material over time. None of this is exotic, but knowing the vocabulary lets you compare two listings honestly instead of guessing.

Types and Categories

Most vanities sort into a few clear categories, and the right one usually picks itself once you know your room. Freestanding vanities sit on the floor and are the default for a reason: they are forgiving of uneven floors, easy to install, and offer the most enclosed storage. Floating (wall-mounted) vanities skip the legs and bolt to the wall, which makes a small room feel larger and a modern bathroom look intentional. The trade-off is that they demand secure anchoring into studs or blocking. We compare these two in detail in our floating vanity roundup.

Then there is sink count. A single-sink vanity suits most bathrooms and leaves more counter space at any given width. A double-sink vanity, which really needs 60 inches or more to work well, is worth it for shared bathrooms where two people get ready at once. Finally, the style families, modern, farmhouse, floating, and traditional white, are mostly about looks rather than function, so settle the practical questions first and let style be the tiebreaker.

How to Choose

Start with measurements, because they rule out more options than anything else. Measure the wall space you have, then subtract for the door swing, the toilet clearance (most codes want about 15 inches from the centerline of the toilet to anything beside it), and any path you need to walk. Write down the maximum width and the maximum depth. In a narrow room, depth is often the real constraint, and that is the number people forget.

Next, find your plumbing rough-in. Look at where the hot and cold supply lines and the drain exit the wall or floor. A vanity with an open back or a center door accommodates existing plumbing easily; a drawer bank may need a notch cut to clear the trap. If you want to move the plumbing to suit a vanity you love, that is fine, but price the plumber in before you decide.

Then think about how you actually use the bathroom. Two people sharing a morning routine lean toward a double sink or at least wide drawers. One person in a small space is usually better served by a single sink and more counter. Decide your storage priority, hidden cabinet space versus open shelving, and whether you need a top included or already own one.

Only now should you set a budget and pick a style. Spending more buys solid-wood construction, stone tops, and soft-close hardware that genuinely last longer, but a mid-range vanity that fits your room perfectly beats an expensive one that does not. Common Mistakes to Avoid

The most expensive mistake is buying on width alone and ignoring the door swing. A 36-inch vanity might fit the wall but block the bathroom door from opening fully, and you will not discover it until the box is on your floor. Map the swing before you buy, not after.

The second is overlooking the plumbing. People fall for a drawer-front vanity, then learn the trap and supply lines run straight through where the drawers should be. Confirm your rough-in location matches the cabinet's interior layout, or budget for moving the plumbing.

Third, assuming the price includes a top and sink. A cabinet-only listing can look like a steal until you add a stone counter, a sink, and a faucet, at which point it is no cheaper than the complete unit you scrolled past. Read what is in the box.

Finally, two quieter errors: picking the wrong faucet hole configuration so the faucet you want will not mount, and choosing particleboard in a humid bathroom. Match the top's drilling to your faucet, and favor solid wood or plywood where steam and splashes are a daily fact of life.

Care and Maintenance

A vanity lives in the most humid room in the house, so the small habits matter more than any deep clean. Wipe standing water off the countertop and around the faucet base after use; water that sits along seams and at the backsplash is what eventually works its way into the cabinet and swells the material. A dry microfiber cloth takes seconds and does most of the protective work.

If you have a natural stone top like marble or granite, reseal it once or twice a year, and skip acidic cleaners (vinegar, citrus) that etch the surface; pH-neutral cleaner is the safe default. Quartz and laminate tops are more forgiving and need only mild soap and water. Whatever the surface, blot spills rather than letting them dry.

For the cabinet itself, check the hinges and drawer slides every few months and tighten any hardware that has loosened with daily use. Keep the area under the sink dry by glancing at the supply lines and trap now and then, because a slow drip will damage a cabinet floor long before you notice a puddle. If you spot a leak, fix it immediately and dry the wood.

Done consistently, none of this takes more than a few minutes a week, and it is the difference between a vanity that still looks new in five years and one with a swollen base and a clouded top.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the standard size of a bathroom vanity?

There is no single standard, but the most common single-sink widths are 24, 30, 36, and 48 inches, and the depth is almost always around 21 inches with a finished height of 32 to 36 inches. A 30-to-36-inch vanity covers most bathrooms. Save 48-inch and wider models for full baths that have the wall space, and step down to 18-to-24 inches for powder rooms and tight corners.

Does a bathroom vanity come with a countertop and sink?

Sometimes, and the listing does not always make it obvious. Many vanities are sold as a complete unit with the top and sink included, but others ship as the cabinet only, leaving you to buy the countertop, sink, and faucet separately. Always read the description and the photos carefully. A cabinet-only price that looks like a bargain can end up costing more once you add a stone top and a sink.

Is a floating vanity harder to install than a freestanding one?

Yes, modestly. A floating, wall-mounted vanity has to be anchored into studs or solid blocking so it can carry its own weight plus a stone top, which means more careful layout and sometimes opening the wall to add blocking. A freestanding vanity simply sits on the floor and is leveled against the wall, so it forgives uneven floors and skips the anchoring puzzle. If you are not comfortable locating studs and checking that the wall can take the load, a freestanding unit is the safer DIY choice.

How much should I spend on a bathroom vanity?

For most bathrooms, a single-sink vanity that includes a top lands somewhere between $300 and $800, and that range buys solid construction that will last. You can spend under $200 for a small cabinet-only or makeup-style unit, and well past $1,500 for a wide double-sink vanity with a stone top and soft-close hardware. Spend more only where it earns its keep: a larger size you actually need, a more durable countertop, or better drawer hardware you will touch every day.

Can I install a bathroom vanity myself?

Often, yes, if you are comfortable with basic tools and a little plumbing. Setting and leveling a freestanding vanity is a manageable weekend job; the part that trips people up is connecting the drain and supply lines and sealing the top to the wall. A floating vanity raises the difficulty because of the anchoring. If your project means moving water lines or the drain, that is the point where most people bring in a plumber, and it is money well spent to avoid a leak inside a finished wall.

Verdict

Choosing a bathroom vanity is mostly a measuring exercise dressed up as a design decision. If you take only one thing from this guide, make it the order of operations: measure your space and door swing, locate your plumbing, decide on one sink or two, confirm whether the top and sink are included, and let style be the last call rather than the first. Do it in that sequence and you sidestep the returns and the surprise plumbing bills that make this purchase feel harder than it is. Also, pair your vanity with the right bathroom mirror. Also, place a sleek soap dispenser on the countertop. Also, hang quality bath towels nearby. Also, mount a towel warmer next to your vanity.

For most bathrooms, a 30-to-36-inch freestanding single-sink vanity with the top included is the safe, sensible default, and it is where we would start if nothing about your room demands otherwise. Go wider, go double, or go floating when your space and routine genuinely call for it.
