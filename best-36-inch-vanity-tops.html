Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Size dictates sink count. A 36-inch top holds one sink with counter space to spare. For two sinks, you need at least 48 inches, which is why several picks here run wider.
  • Most tops sit on engineered wood. Every vanity in this guide uses an MDF cabinet. That keeps the price down, but you need to keep standing water off the surface and seal any cut edges.
  • Floating vs freestanding changes install. Floating vanities mount to wall studs and look modern, while freestanding ones sit on the floor and go in faster. Pick based on your wall and your comfort with tools.
  • Price tracks finish and extras. The picks below run from about $470 to $1,446, and the jump usually buys you a second sink, a smart mirror, or a more durable top.
  • Check your rough-in before buying. Measure the distance from the wall to your drain and your supply lines so the new top and cabinet line up with existing plumbing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best material for a 36-inch vanity top?

Most 36-inch vanity tops sit on engineered wood (MDF) cabinets, and the top itself is usually a sealed stone-look or resin surface. Engineered wood holds up well in a bathroom as long as you seal any cut edges and wipe up standing water. If you want the longest life and have the budget, a quartz or natural-stone top resists scratches and stains better, but the MDF-based picks in this guide handle daily use fine for most bathrooms.

Will a 36-inch vanity top fit one or two sinks?

A 36-inch top fits one sink comfortably with usable counter space on each side. Two sinks at 36 inches feels cramped, so manufacturers usually start double-sink layouts at 48 inches and up. If you need two basins, look at the 48-inch picks in this guide. If a single sink works for you, a 36-inch top like the WindBay gives you more open counter for daily items.

Are floating vanity tops harder to install than freestanding ones?

Floating vanities take a bit more care because you mount them to wall studs rather than resting them on the floor. You need to locate the studs, set the cabinet at the right height, and confirm the wall can carry the load. Freestanding picks like the T4TREAM and the 48-inch budget vanity skip that step since they sit on legs. If you are comfortable using a stud finder and a level, a floating 36-inch vanity is a weekend job; if not, budget for a plumber or handyman.
