Things to Know Before You Buy

  • Power comes first. You turn off the breaker, not just the wall switch, and you confirm the wires are dead with a voltage tester before your hands go near the box.
  • Most jobs take about an hour. Swapping a fixture into an existing electrical box is straightforward. Running a new circuit from scratch is a different job that needs a licensed electrician.
  • Wires match by color. Black ties to black, white to white, and the bare copper or green wire connects to ground. If your wall wires show no colors, stop and call a pro.
  • You need a GFCI-protected circuit. Bathroom code in the US requires ground-fault protection because the room mixes water and electricity.
  • Budget around $20 in supplies for wire nuts, electrical tape, and anchors, on top of the cost of the light itself.

Learning how to install bathroom vanity light fixtures is one of the few electrical jobs a confident DIYer can finish in a single afternoon. If you are replacing an old light over your mirror with a brighter LED bar or a Hollywood-style strip, you already have the hardest part done for you: the electrical box and the wiring are sitting right there in the wall. You connect three wires, fasten the fixture, and flip the switch. That is the whole job for most bathrooms.

This guide walks you through the swap in five concrete steps, from killing the power at the breaker to testing the new light. You will also see where the work crosses a line that calls for a licensed electrician, because some situations are not worth the risk. Read the safety notes, gather your supplies, and give yourself an uninterrupted hour. By the end you will have even, flattering light at the mirror and the satisfaction of having wired it yourself.

Total time: About 1 hour

Estimated cost: $20 in supplies (light fixture sold separately)

What You'll Need

  • Supplies: Wire connectors (wire nuts), electrical tape, mounting screws and wall anchors, and your new vanity light fixture
  • Tools: Non-contact voltage tester, Phillips and flathead screwdrivers, wire strippers, a level, and a small step stool

Step 1: Shut off the power and confirm the circuit is dead

Before you do anything else when you install a bathroom vanity light, go to your electrical panel and switch off the breaker that feeds the bathroom. Flipping the wall switch alone is not enough, because the fixture box stays live even with the switch off. If your breakers are not labeled, turn the existing light on, then flip breakers one at a time until the light goes dark. That tells you which one controls the circuit.

Back at the fixture, hold a non-contact voltage tester near the wires after you expose them in Step 2. A tester that stays silent and dark confirms the circuit is dead. Test it first on a wire you know is live, like a nearby outlet, so you trust the reading. This thirty-second check is the habit that keeps you out of the emergency room, so never skip it.

Leave a note on the panel or tape over the breaker so nobody flips it back on while you work. If you share the house, tell whoever is home that the bathroom power is off on purpose.

Step 2: Remove the old fixture and inspect the wiring

Unscrew the mounting nuts or screws that hold the old light to the wall and lower the fixture gently. Support its weight with one hand so it does not hang from the wires and strain the connections. Once it is free, unscrew the wire nuts and separate the fixture wires from the wires coming out of the wall.

Take a moment to look at what you uncovered. You should see a black wire, a white wire, and a bare copper or green ground wire feeding into a metal or plastic electrical box. Check that the box is screwed firmly to a stud or brace and does not wobble. Look for scorch marks, cracked insulation, or corrosion, since any of those signs means you should stop and bring in an electrician before you install a bathroom vanity light on that circuit.

If the wires are stiff or nicked at the ends, snip off the damaged tip and strip back about three-quarters of an inch of fresh insulation with your wire strippers. Clean, undamaged copper makes a far better connection than reusing a frayed wire end.

Step 3: Mount the new bracket to the electrical box

Your new light comes with its own mounting bracket, often called a crossbar or strap. Set the old bracket aside and screw the new one to the electrical box using the screws in the kit. Manufacturers size their brackets to their own fixtures, so always use the hardware that ships in the box rather than the parts you just removed.

Hold a level against the bracket as you tighten the screws. A vanity light that sits even a couple of degrees off looks crooked the moment the fixture goes on, and it is far easier to fix now than after everything is wired. Snug the screws down so the bracket sits flat against the box and does not flex when you press on it.

If your fixture mounts directly to the drywall rather than the box, mark the screw holes, drill pilot holes, and tap in the wall anchors that came with the light. Anchors keep the screws from pulling out of the drywall over time as the fixture takes on the weight of the bulbs and glass shades.

Step 4: Connect the wires by color

Now you join the fixture wires to the wall wires, and the colors do the work for you. Twist the black fixture wire together with the black wall wire and cap them with a wire nut. Do the same for the two white wires. Then connect the fixture ground, which is either a green wire or a bare copper wire, to the bare copper or green ground in the box.

Twist each wire nut clockwise until it grips firmly, then tug each wire to make sure none slips out. A loose connection is the most common cause of a flickering light or a dead fixture, so this is where patience pays off. Wrap a turn of electrical tape around the base of each nut for extra security, then fold the connected wires neatly so they tuck back into the box without pinching.

If your wall wires have no color coding, or you find more wires than you expected, do not guess. Older homes sometimes run switch loops or shared neutrals that change the rules. That is the point to call a licensed electrician rather than install a bathroom vanity light on wiring you cannot read with confidence.

Step 5: Secure the fixture and test the light

Gently push the connected wires into the electrical box, then lift the fixture onto the bracket and line up the mounting holes. Thread the decorative nuts or screws by hand first so you do not cross-thread them, then tighten until the fixture sits flush against the wall. Do not overtighten, since too much force can crack a metal backplate or a glass shade.

Install the bulbs your fixture calls for, then go back to the panel and switch the breaker on. Return to the bathroom and flip the wall switch. The light should come on steady and bright. If you installed a dimmable LED bar, run it up and down the full range to check for flicker or buzz at low settings.

If nothing happens, turn the breaker back off and recheck your wire nuts, since a loose connection is the usual culprit. Once the light works, wipe your fingerprints off the shade and you are done. You now know how to install a bathroom vanity light from start to finish, a skill that carries over to almost any light fixture in the house.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The biggest mistake people make when they install a bathroom vanity light is trusting the wall switch instead of the breaker. The switch only breaks one leg of the circuit, so the box stays live. Always cut power at the panel and verify with a tester. Skipping that step is how DIYers end up with a shock or a melted screwdriver.

A second common slip is forgetting the ground wire. The black and white wires make the light turn on, so it is tempting to ignore the bare copper. The ground is your safety path if a fault ever sends current into the metal housing, and in a wet room that path matters even more. Connect it every time.

People also overtighten the mounting hardware and crack a glass shade or warp a thin backplate. Snug is enough. Another frequent error is mounting the fixture crooked because nobody checked it with a level before wiring, which forces a full redo to straighten it.

Finally, some homeowners try to add a brand new light where no box exists, fishing wires through the wall themselves. That crosses into new-circuit territory and often violates local code. If there is no existing fixture box, hire a licensed electrician rather than improvise. Knowing when to stop is part of doing the job right.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I install a bathroom vanity light myself without an electrician?

Yes, if you are replacing an existing fixture and the box and wiring are already in place. Swapping one light for another is a beginner-friendly job as long as you cut the power at the breaker and connect the wires by color. You should call a licensed electrician if there is no existing box, the wiring looks damaged, or your local code requires a permit for the work.

How long does it take to install a vanity light?

A straight fixture swap takes about an hour for someone doing it for the first time, and closer to 30 minutes once you have done a few. Most of that time goes into removing the old light, mounting the new bracket, and double-checking the wire connections. Running a brand new circuit takes far longer and belongs to an electrician.

Which wire goes where on a vanity light?

Match the colors. Connect the black fixture wire to the black wall wire, the white to the white, and the green or bare copper ground wire to the ground in the box. If your wall wires have no clear colors, or you find extra wires, stop and have an electrician identify them before you connect anything.

Do bathroom vanity lights need a special box or GFCI protection?

The fixture mounts to a standard electrical box, but US code requires the bathroom circuit to have ground-fault protection, either at the outlet or the breaker. If your bathroom was wired to current code, that protection is already in place. If you are unsure, have an electrician confirm the circuit is GFCI protected before you rely on it.

How high should I mount a vanity light above the mirror?

A common guideline puts the fixture about 75 to 80 inches from the floor, or roughly 3 inches above the top of the mirror. That height casts light onto your face rather than the top of your head, which reduces shadows when you shave or apply makeup. Adjust within a couple of inches to suit your mirror size and ceiling height.
